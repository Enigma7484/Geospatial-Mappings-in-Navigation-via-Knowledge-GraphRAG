Personalized route recommendation is usually evaluated with clean navigation logs, explicit user histories, or proprietary trajectory data. This paper studies a weaker but practical setting: route ranking from OpenStreetMap (OSM) data and public GPS-derived movement signals. We present a working route-ranking system that generates OSM route candidates, extracts interpretable route features, constructs contextual pseudo-history profiles, and reranks candidates using profile, prompt, or hybrid scoring. The paper separates two questions that are often conflated in prototypes: whether noisy public traces can be reconstructed well enough to serve as historical movement evidence, and whether profile-aware reranking improves over non-personalized baselines. On an OSM public-trace probe, timestamp coverage and GPS-to-route proximity are strong, but strict reconstruction usability fails because route-distance ratio remains high. To move toward a fair public benchmark, we add a Porto taxi trajectory evaluation with OSM driving-route candidates, feature/path oracles, bootstrap confidence intervals, and NASR-style path-overlap metrics. In this 50-query Porto prototype, a vehicle-aware profile ranker improves over shortest-distance and a generic profile on Hit@1, Hit@3, MRR, NDCG@3, and mean feature distance, while path-level metrics show that map matching and candidate generation remain bottlenecks. The contribution is therefore a transparent, reproducible benchmark framework for preference-aware OSM route ranking, not a premature claim that public GPS traces are clean per-user histories.
